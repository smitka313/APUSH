Reasoning Processes

  • Comparison: similarities, differences, significance.
  • Causation: causes, effects, short/long-term, primary/secondary.
  • Continuity & Change: what changed, what persisted, why it matters.

MoveWhat to askSentence starter

Command Terms
CommandWhat earns points
IdentifyGive the specific answer.
DescribeGive relevant characteristics with enough detail.
ExplainShow how or why using evidence and reasoning.
CompareAddress similarities and/or differences.
EvaluateMake a judgment about extent/significance with a line of reasoning.
Point-Earning Answer Examples
WeakStronger
The New Deal helped people.The New Deal expanded federal responsibility for economic security through Social Security and the Wagner Act, though it did not fully end the Depression.
Slavery caused the Civil War.Conflict over the expansion of slavery intensified sectional party realignment, as seen in the Kansas-Nebraska Act and election of 1860.

DBQ / LEQ / SAQ Writing Center

1 SAQ + 1 DBQ + 1 LEQ per unitRubric-drivenAPUSH skills
Universal formula: Claim + specific evidence + reasoning. Never let evidence sit alone.
Rubric Quick Reference
TaskPoint-earning habits
SAQUse ACE for each part: answer the question, cite specific evidence, explain the connection. No thesis required.
DBQThesis, contextualization, document evidence, outside evidence, sourcing/HIPP, complexity.
LEQThesis, contextualization, specific evidence, historical reasoning process, complexity.
Command Term Reminders
  • Identify: name it clearly.
  • Describe: give relevant details.
  • Explain: connect cause and effect using historical reasoning.
  • Evaluate the extent: make a defensible judgment, not a yes/no claim.

CED Audit: Unit-by-Unit Test-Prep Additions

Checked against CEDUse before the exam
Purpose: This section tightens each unit around what students must be able to explain, compare, contextualize, source, and argue on the APUSH exam. It removes the “just memorize facts” approach and turns each unit into CED-aligned test preparation.
Unit 1: 1491-1607 - must not miss
  • Native diversity: Students should compare societies by environment: maize agriculture, mobile hunting, mixed agriculture/hunting, and coastal resource economies.
  • European motives: Connect trade, empire, Christianity, and maritime technology to exploration and conquest.
  • Columbian Exchange: Explain consequences, not just examples: disease, population collapse, new crops/animals, environmental change, and coerced labor.
  • Spanish colonial systems: Link encomienda, caste hierarchy, slavery, and cultural blending/resistance.
Best exam move: Use Unit 1 evidence to contextualize later colonial labor systems, racial hierarchy, and Atlantic exchange.
Unit 2: 1607-1754 - must not miss
  • Regional colonies: Compare New England, Chesapeake, Middle Colonies, and Southern colonies by labor, economy, religion, settlement, and social structure.
  • European imperial systems: Spanish, French, Dutch, and British colonies differed in settlement patterns and Native relations.
  • Atlantic economy: Mercantilism, Navigation Acts, transatlantic trade, Middle Passage, and salutary neglect should be connected to later imperial tension.
  • Colonial culture: Enlightenment, Great Awakening, print culture, self-government, and slavery all shaped colonial identity.
Common mistake: Saying “the colonies became American” without explaining specific causes such as self-government, religious movements, trade, and distance from Britain.
Unit 3: 1754-1800 - must not miss
  • Revolution causes: Seven Years' War debt, British taxation, imperial enforcement, Enlightenment/republican ideas, and colonial resistance.
  • Revolutionary ideals: Explain both change and limits for women, enslaved people, free Black communities, Native peoples, and loyalists.
  • Government change: Articles of Confederation weaknesses, Shays' Rebellion, Constitutional Convention, Federalist/Anti-Federalist debates, Bill of Rights.
  • Early Republic: Hamilton's financial plan, party formation, Whiskey Rebellion, foreign policy neutrality, Alien and Sedition Acts.
Best exam move: For “turning point” prompts, argue that 1776 changed political legitimacy, while 1787-1791 changed federal power.
Unit 4: 1800-1848 - must not miss
  • Federal power and foreign policy: Jefferson, Louisiana Purchase, Marbury, War of 1812, Monroe Doctrine.
  • Market Revolution: Transportation, communication, factories, commercial agriculture, wage labor, class change, and gender ideology.
  • Jacksonian democracy: Expanded white male suffrage and party politics, but continued exclusion of women, Native Americans, and African Americans.
  • Reform: Second Great Awakening, abolition, temperance, education, prisons/asylums, women's rights, and utopian communities.
  • Slavery and resistance: Cotton kingdom, slave codes, Nat Turner, abolitionism, free Black activism.
Common mistake: Calling the period democratic without qualifying who gained and who lost power.
Unit 5: 1844-1877 - must not miss
  • Expansion and slavery: Manifest Destiny, Mexican-American War, Wilmot Proviso, Compromise of 1850, Kansas-Nebraska Act, Dred Scott, and Republican Party formation.
  • Failure of compromise: Explain why sectional differences became irreconcilable by 1860.
  • Civil War: Union/Confederate advantages, emancipation, federal power, wartime economy, and social changes.
  • Reconstruction: 13th, 14th, 15th Amendments; Freedmen's Bureau; Black political participation; sharecropping; Black Codes; KKK; Compromise of 1877.
Best exam move: For Reconstruction, write a balanced thesis: constitutional change was dramatic, but social/economic equality was limited by violence, racism, and labor dependency.
Unit 6: 1865-1898 - must not miss
  • Westward expansion: Railroads, mining, ranching, farming, federal land policy, warfare, reservations, Dawes Act, and destruction of Native sovereignty.
  • Industrial capitalism: Technology, corporations, trusts, vertical/horizontal integration, laissez-faire, Social Darwinism, Gospel of Wealth.
  • Labor conflict: Knights of Labor, AFL, Haymarket, Homestead, Pullman; connect to wage labor, unsafe conditions, and employer power.
  • Immigration and urbanization: New immigrants, nativism, Chinese Exclusion, settlement houses, Social Gospel, political machines.
  • New South and politics: sharecropping, crop-lien system, Jim Crow, Plessy, Populism, currency/tariff debates, limited federal regulation.
Common mistake: Treating the Gilded Age as only industrial growth; AP prompts often ask about inequality, labor, government, migration, and reform.
Unit 7: 1890-1945 - must not miss
  • Imperialism: Markets, strategy, racial ideology, missionaries, Spanish-American War, Philippines, Open Door, Roosevelt Corollary, anti-imperialism.
  • Progressivism: Muckrakers, regulatory reform, women's suffrage, direct democracy, conservation, labor protections, and limits on race.
  • World War I: neutrality, entry, mobilization, civil liberties, Wilson's Fourteen Points, League debate, isolationism.
  • 1920s: consumer culture, mass media, Harlem Renaissance, immigration restriction, Red Scare, religious conflict, gender change.
  • Great Depression/New Deal: causes, relief/recovery/reform, Social Security, Wagner Act, critics, court-packing, and limits.
  • World War II: mobilization, women/minorities, internment, wartime diplomacy, atomic bomb, U.S. global leadership.
Best exam move: For New Deal prompts, evaluate change in federal responsibility while acknowledging limits and continuity in capitalism/racial inequality.
Unit 8: 1945-1980 - must not miss
  • Cold War: containment, Truman Doctrine, Marshall Plan, NATO, Korea, arms race, Cuba, Vietnam, detente.
  • Domestic anticommunism: loyalty programs, HUAC, McCarthyism, civil liberties concerns.
  • Postwar society: suburbs, Sun Belt, baby boom, consumer culture, highways, conformity, poverty, and exclusion.
  • Civil rights: Brown, Montgomery, sit-ins, Freedom Rides, March on Washington, Civil Rights Act, Voting Rights Act, Black Power, resistance.
  • Rights movements and reform: Great Society, feminism, Latino/a activism, Native activism, environmentalism, LGBTQ activism, youth culture.
  • Backlash and crisis: Vietnam protests, stagflation, Watergate, conservative movement growth.
Common mistake: Treating civil rights as only African American activism; the CED expects broader rights movements and social change.
Unit 9: 1980-Present - must not miss
  • Conservatism: Reagan, tax cuts, deregulation, reduced government rhetoric, religious conservatism, social policy debates.
  • End of Cold War: Reagan foreign policy, Soviet decline, post-Cold War U.S. leadership.
  • Changing economy: deindustrialization, service/information economy, globalization, free trade, technology, inequality.
  • Migration and identity: post-1965 immigration effects, Sun Belt growth, multiculturalism, political debates over immigration.
  • 21st-century challenges: September 11, War on Terror, Patriot Act, Afghanistan/Iraq, Great Recession, polarization.
Best exam move: Connect Unit 9 to earlier themes: federal power, immigration, foreign intervention, market change, and national identity.

Last-Week Plan

  2. Do one SAQ set daily.
  3. Outline DBQ/LEQ prompts before writing full essays.
  4. Practice stimulus MCQ: source, context, eliminate.

High-Scoring Sentence Stems

  • “This evidence supports the argument because...”
  • “Although ___ changed, ___ continued because...”
  • “The most significant cause was ___ because...”
  • “The author’s purpose limits the source because...”
  • “A broader context for this development was...”
Common score killers: vague thesis, no outside evidence, summary instead of sourcing, listing facts without reasoning, and not answering all SAQ parts.
